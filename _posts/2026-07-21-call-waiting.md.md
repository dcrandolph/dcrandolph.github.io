It is raining. Serious Florida rain. We cannot go outside today, so we eat our lunches in a big circle on the floor of the Assembly Room. We try and almost succeed not to spill. 

What should we do now? 

We aren't allowed to play TV Tag or Kitty Wants a Corner inside, ever since Billy Darrell ran too fast around the corner by the Boy's Bathroom and slipped and broke his leg. We don't run inside. We *walk* inside. There are Inside Games and there are Outside Games. 

So we play an Inside Game. We play Telephone. 

Tina Devasher whispers something to Sadie Davenport. Sadie whispers to James Lollis. And so on, and so on, and so on, like that shampoo commercial. By the time the whisper gets to me it is absolute wonderful gibberish and everyone in the half of the circle who has heard the whisper is laughing. Everyone who hasn't heard can't think of anything more exciting than what is about to be shared with them. That's the game.

I pass on the message, the best I can, not really wanting clarity as much as silliness. 

By the time it gets all the way around to Simon Morton, he shares a sentence that is nothing like what Tina or Sadie or James or anybody said, nothing like what I heard or passed along. And everybody is laughing, and we do it again. Nobody even cares that we can't go outside. That's the game.

When recess ends we go back to our desks and learn the importance of Factual Accuracy and Good Data. And we keep learning it for the rest of forever. Nobody laughs.

But when the rain stops and we can go outside again, something beautiful and big happens, in the sky or in the heart or both, _and_ both, and we share it with somebody else, we can't help it, filtered through what we think and feel and have been taught is okay and important. That's what everybody is doing, all the time. That's the game.

We don't judge how good the messenger is. We wait and we hear and we laugh and we share, knowing that the truth of the thing is not in the words but in the moment. That's the game.
